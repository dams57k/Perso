Study Design

A retrospective cohort study enrolling school-age children (ages 7–16) who used overnight orthokeratology between 1998 and 2013. Controls were age- and refraction-matched spectacle wearers from the same clinic.

Key question: Does overnight Ortho-K slow myopia progression over a period much longer than the typical 1–3 year studies — specifically up to 12 years?

How Ortho-K Works

The biological and optical mechanism behind overnight corneal reshaping

🔬

The Reverse Geometry Lens

Orthokeratology lenses have a much flatter central base curve than the secondary curve. This creates a differential pressure profile across the cornea during sleep.

Pressure mechanics: Positive pushing pressure against the central cornea + negative pulling pressure against the mid-peripheral cornea → epithelial cells redistribute outward, flattening the centre.
👁️

Step-by-Step Process

1

Lens worn overnight (6–8 hrs)

Reverse geometry lens placed on eye before sleep; patient wears during the night

2

Central epithelium thins

Positive pressure flattens the central cornea by redistributing epithelial cells toward the mid-periphery

3

Plateau-shaped cornea forms

Light is now refracted simultaneously onto both the macula and the mid-peripheral retina

4

Peripheral myopic defocus created

Mid-peripheral retina receives myopic (not hyperopic) defocus, which is believed to inhibit eye growth signals

5

Axial elongation slows

Reduced peripheral hyperopic defocus → less stimulus for axial elongation → slower myopia progression

⚠️

Why Peripheral Defocus Matters

Hyperopic peripheral defocus (light focuses behind the peripheral retina) is commonly found in myopic children and is believed to encourage eye growth. Ortho-K flips this signal toward myopic defocus, potentially stabilising axial length.


The lens used in this study: Dreimlens® (Macro Vision Corp., Taiwan) — a fluorosilicone acrylate material, Dk 127, diameter 10.4–11.2 mm, central thickness 0.22 mm.

Risk Factors & Predictors

What baseline characteristics influence Ortho-K outcomes?

🎯

Significant Predictor: Astigmatism

In the GEE statistical model, initial astigmatism power ≥ −1.5 D was the only factor significantly associated with greater refractive error change over both 8 and 12 years of follow-up.

Why? Standard spherical 4-zone Ortho-K lenses may cause incomplete myopic defocus of the peripheral retina in high-astigmatism eyes, reducing the myopia-slowing effect. Toric Ortho-K designs were not available during the study period.

The J0 power vector (horizontal astigmatism axis) with greater negative values was also independently associated with increased refractive change (p < 0.001 in both models).

✅

Non-Significant Factors

These baseline factors were NOT significantly associated with refractive error progression:

Starting age (≤10 vs >10 yrs) Initial myopic power (<5D vs ≥5D) Lens replacement frequency

About 70% of subjects started Ortho-K after age 10. About 82% had stable lens replacement (average <2 years). Neither significantly affected outcomes.

📌

Study Limitations to Note

!

Refraction over lens (not axial length)

Myopia progression was measured via back optic zone radius changes, not direct axial length measurement

!

Retrospective design

Neither examiner nor patient was masked to treatment group — potential for bias

!

Low retention after 8 years

Only ~14% of subjects remained after year 8; results beyond this point should be interpreted cautiously

!

Standard lens only

Spherical 4-zone design only; results limited to children with low-to-moderate myopia and low astigmatism

Safety Profile

Long-term adverse events over the 12-year study period

🛡️

Overall Safety: Clinically Acceptable

The study followed 66 children with overnight contact lens wear for up to 13 years. The safety record was reassuring, with only minor, reversible events reported.

8
Mild superficial punctate keratopathy cases
2
Mild corneal erosion cases
0
Severe complications (e.g. corneal ulcer)
✅ All minor events fully resolved after 1–2 weeks of lens discontinuation (and lens re-design in one patient). No permanent damage was observed.
💊

Comparison with Atropine

Topical atropine is another leading myopia control option. While effective, it carries notable side effects at higher doses:

Photophobia Allergic reactions Rebound effect at high doses Ortho-K: no systemic side effects Ortho-K: reversible

Rinse with sterile saline

Preserved sterile saline solution after cleaning

3

Replace every 1–2 years
