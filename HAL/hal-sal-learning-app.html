Study Design

What was tested?

A double-masked randomised clinical trial at the Eye Hospital of Wenzhou Medical University, China (July 2018–Oct 2020). Children were randomly assigned 1:1:1 to one of three spectacle lens types and followed for 2 years with measurements every 6 months.

Primary question: Do spectacle lenses with aspherical lenslets slow myopia progression — and does higher asphericity produce a stronger dose-dependent effect?
Participants

Who was enrolled?

Age: 8–13 yrs (mean 10.4)

Myopia: −0.75 D to −4.75 D

Astigmatism: ≤ −1.50 D

BCVA: ≤ 0.05 logMAR each eye

Ethnicity: Chinese (Wenzhou, China)

Prior myopia Rx: None allowed

Measurements: Cycloplegic SER via Topcon KR-800 (≥30 min after cyclopentolate 1%). Axial length via Lenstar LS 900, mean of 5 readings.
Context

Why this matters

Myopia prevalence is projected to reach alarming levels by 2050. High myopia increases risk of retinal detachment, macular degeneration, and glaucoma. This trial tests a specific design feature — lenslet asphericity — and whether varying it creates a dose-response relationship in myopia control.

The Three Lens Types

Understanding HAL, SAL, and SVL — what they are and how they differ

HAL
Highly Aspherical Lenslets
Greater curvature deviation from sphere. Strongest peripheral myopic defocus signal.
SAL
Slightly Aspherical Lenslets
Moderate lenslet curvature. Intermediate defocus signal.
SVL
Single-Vision Lenses
Standard corrective lenses. Control group — no myopia control feature.
Mechanism

How aspherical lenslets work

1

Central correction zone

The centre provides standard distance correction — the child sees clearly, just like single-vision lenses.

2

Concentric aspherical lenslet rings

Surrounding the centre are concentric rings of small aspherical lenslets, visible as subtle rings under certain lighting.

3

Peripheral myopic defocus

Lenslets focus light in front of the peripheral retina (myopic defocus), removing the hyperopic defocus signal that drives eye elongation.

4

Dose-response by asphericity

Greater asphericity (HAL > SAL) produces a stronger defocus signal. This is the core hypothesis — confirmed by the results.

5

Axial elongation slows

Reduced hyperopic peripheral defocus → weaker growth stimulus → slower axial elongation → slower myopia progression.

Clinical implication

Key takeaway

For HAL, full-time wear (≥12 hrs/day) substantially improves outcomes. Clinicians should counsel families accordingly. The improving adherence over time suggests initial adjustment barriers are transient.

HAL full-time: 67% SER reduction HAL part-time: 37% SER reduction SAL: no wear-time effect

Adverse events, study strengths, and caveats when interpreting findings

Device-related discontinuations

One SVL wearer had punctate corneal epithelial defects; one SAL wearer was hit by a ball causing frame breakage and bruising. Neither was device-related. No significant difference between groups (p = 0.59).

✓ Both HAL and SAL demonstrated a clean safety profile over 2 years in children aged 8–13.
Strengths

What makes this a strong study?

✓

Double-masked RCT

Examiners and participants masked to HAL vs SAL allocation. Complete masking achieved between HAL and SAL (not possible vs SVL due to visible lenslets).

✓

Low dropout rate (6.5%)

Only 13 of 170 enrolled did not complete 2 years. Dropout reasons were unrelated to the study devices.

✓

Short recruitment window (~2 months)

Reduced confounding from seasonal variation in myopia progression, a known source of bias.

✓

Cycloplegic SER + axial length

Both refractive and structural endpoints measured, providing a comprehensive picture of myopia control.

Limitations

What should temper the conclusions?

Chinese cohort only Self-reported wear time 18-mo visit delayed 3 wks (COVID) Outdoor time not measured Right eye only analysed Essilor funded & co-authored

Results from a high-myopia-prevalence Chinese cohort may not generalise globally. Self-reported wearing hours may over-estimate actual wear. The 18-month visit was delayed ~3 weeks due to COVID-19, which also interrupted school attendance for 4 months, potentially affecting myopia progression via increased indoor time. Essilor International (maker of HAL/SAL lenses) co-funded the study and had employees as co-authors — a notable conflict of interest to be aware of.
